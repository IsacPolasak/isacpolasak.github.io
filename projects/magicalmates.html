Magical Mates

A magical companion for stories, routines, and connections.

Project Overview

Product: Magical Mates is a smart stuffed animal and app that helps children build routines and spark imagination. Parents use the app to send voice messages and stories, which are delivered through the plush toy using AI-generated audio.

Problem: Parents want to reduce screen time while staying connected and supporting routines. Most tools lack emotional warmth or imaginative play.

Goal: Create a magical, screen-free experience that helps families stay connected, build routines, and spark imagination through smart, voice-enabled plush toys.

Empathize

Understanding The Problem and The User

Before conducting research, I assumed parents wanted their kids to have a screen-free way to stay connected with their parents and make routines easier.

Through user interviews and observation, I confirmed that many struggled to balance busy schedules with quality bonding moments. For example, the person whom the persona is based on – a nurse and mom of a five-year-old – shared how difficult it was to maintain a consistent bedtime routine when she worked late shifts. She wanted a comforting, familiar presence her child could rely on, even when she couldn’t be there in person.

In speaking with parents and children, I discovered that emotional connection was just as important as structure. Parents valued tools that helped with routines, and children responded best to interactions that felt personal with a dash of magic.

“I just want my daughter to feel like I’m there with her, even when I can’t be home at bedtime.”
— Mom of five-year-old
“My son listens so much better when it’s coming from a character he loves. If it feels like play, it doesn’t feel like a routine.”
— Dad of four-year-old
“Mommy’s too busy to say good night.”
— Boy, age 7
Pain Points

Screen Time: Parents want to reduce their children’s screen time but struggle to find engaging, screen-free alternatives that still foster connection.

Impersonality: Existing routine and storytelling tools feel impersonal and lack emotional warmth or imaginative play.

Boredom: Kids lose interest in static toys or apps that don’t feel “alive” or personalized to them.

Key Takeaways:
  1. • Customers are short on time in the mornings.
  2. • Long lines and menu explanations delay service.
  3. • Both customers and staff are frustrated during rush hours.

Define

Framing The Problem

Through research and interviews, it became clear that families face a shared challenge: busy parents struggle to maintain comforting routines and meaningful connection with their children during hectic schedules, while children miss consistent, engaging interactions that feel personal. Parents want to reduce screen time but lack tools that bring emotional warmth and imaginative play into daily routines.

The design challenge is to create a solution that supports families in building nurturing, screen-free routines, fosters emotional connection through playful and personalized interactions, and fits naturally into the busy lives of both parents and children. Caregivers and kids need an inviting, easy-to-use way to stay connected and maintain comforting routines, even when they can’t be together in person.

Developing The Persona

From user interviews, a common theme emerged: busy parents struggle to maintain comforting routines while managing hectic schedules. Many described the challenge of balancing screen time reduction with meaningful connection. One such parent inspired the persona of Christina.

Christina is a busy nurse and mom of a five-year-old who juggles late shifts and family time. She wants her child to have a familiar, comforting presence that supports daily routines, but often finds it difficult to be physically present. Samantha values simple, screen-free solutions that help her stay connected and ease daily routines without adding complexity.

Mapping The User Journey

Looking at the overall journey, it’s clear that parents like Christina juggle busy schedules while wanting to create meaningful, screen-free connections with their children. From discovering the app and connecting the plush toy, to recording, organizing, and sending personalized messages, the process supports building comforting routines. There are several opportunities to improve usability, such as adding onboarding tutorials, enabling message previews and re-recording, organizing messages into folders, and allowing users to choose between real or AI-generated voices to enhance the emotional connection.

Problem Statements

Considering Christina’s goals, pain points, and context as a busy mom, I developed a set of “How Might We” questions. These guided my ideation process and ensured the solution stayed focused on helping her maintain meaningful, screen-free connections with her child through the Magical Mates plush application.

How might we...

• Make it easy for parents to maintain comforting routines remotely?

• Encourage children to engage with the plush toy as a magical, screen-free companion?

• Enable parents to organize and manage voice messages effortlessly, even on tight schedules?

• Support emotional connection by making interactions feel personal and playful?

Ideate

Paper Wireframes

When I started sketching, my goal wasn’t perfection. It was to get ideas down and see what clicked. I created a few versions of each page, each exploring different layouts and flows. Then I highlighted the strongest elements on each page and combined them into a refined concept. The accompanying figure shows this process for the Favorite Audios page, where I starred the key pieces and merged them into the version that became part of my wireframe.

Digital Wireframes

After sketching out a paper wireframe I was happy with, I started building a digital version. This helped me clean things up, get a better feel for spacing and flow, and bring the layout closer to how it might actually look on a screen.

Prototype

Low-Fidelity Prototype Click to open

After finalizing the wireframes, I began connecting everything in Figma to map out the main user flows. I focused on two of the core actions: broadcasting a message and creating or broadcasting a story. These flows were central to the experience, so mapping them out early helped me see how users would move through the app and where any friction might occur.

While building the low-fidelity prototype, I kept the layout minimal and the navigation straightforward, making sure each step in both flows connected smoothly. Since visuals and content were still in progress, I prioritized structure and usability to ensure the foundation was solid before layering on detail.

Usability Study

I did two usability studies. The first one influenced the design evolution from wireframes to mockups. The second, using a high-fidelity prototype, pinpointed areas in the mockups that needed improvement.

Round 1 findings

• Users wanted to find list of favorited audios easier.

• Users wanted to replay previously played stories

Round 2 findings

• Users wanted to broadcast directly from Saved Messages.

• Users wanted to be able to share saved audios and stories.

High-Fidelity Prototype Click to open

When moving from the low-fidelity to the high-fidelity prototype, I focused on reorganizing certain structures, especially around the story creation flow. Based on feedback from the usability study, I realized I had initially overlooked features such as previously played stories, so I incorporated that into the high-fidelity version. I also added details such as age range and other contextual information to make each story more tailored and informative. These updates helped create a more complete and intuitive experience that better reflected real user needs.

Sample Improvements from User Feedback

The first usability study showed the need for a favorite audio section, and so that was added. Users also wanted previously played stories, which was added as well, as well as some other non-major changes such as button layout.

The second usability study proved the need for age ranges in stories, and an easier way to view saved messages, in addition to sharing, broadcasting, and playing messages directly from the saved messages section.

Test

Gathering Feedback

After building the high-fidelity prototype, I tested it with users to evaluate how well two of the main flows – broadcasting a message and creating or broadcasting a story — functioned in practice. I observed participants as they navigated through key tasks, noting where the structure supported their expectations and where it caused confusion. Feedback highlighted that much of the functionality felt intuitive. Another emphasized how design choices, such as color helped reinforce clarity. Additional feedback was taken in order to help polish and tidy up the high-fidelity prototype.

What Users Said
“The background colors changing when clicking different options makes it easier to understand the separation and where in the app I am holding.””
— Tester 1
“Some of the functionality is super familiar, like broadcasting a message or listening to a story. It was extremely intuitive.”
— Tester 2

Conclusion

Final Thoughts

Reflecting on the process, I learned a lot more about Figma than I expected, especially how to use overlays and build more advanced interactions in the prototype. I also improved my icon design skills, learning how to create custom icons that matched the tone of the app. Lastly, I also gained a better sense of how to balance creativity with usability, making sure every design choice served a clear purpose. These small details ended up having a big impact on how polished and intuitive the final product felt.

Accessibility considerations

• Ensured color choices met WCAG contrast ratio guidelines to support accessibility for users with visual impairments.

• Designed with accessibility in mind by using large text and buttons to support users with visual impairments or motor challenges.

• Included captions for all recorded messages to ensure accessibility for users who are deaf or hard of hearing.

Next Steps

• Designing and adding the “Set Routine” portion of the application.

• Add more options and ways to organize saved messages, along with a filtering system for them.

• Conduct more research on parents to see what additional pain points they would like to see solved.
